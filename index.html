Strategy Playbook

Daya

How to turn stablecoin payments into Africa's first supply chain finance product
Prepared for Ayomide, Daya Date July 2026

The opportunity you are already sitting on

You have the three things that make supply chain finance work in Africa and almost nobody else has all of them together:

  1. Deep USDC and USDT liquidity. You mentioned it yourself on X. You can move between Naira and stablecoins at scale.
  2. A no-interest credit facility that already bridges Naira and stablecoins for your users.
  3. Direct relationships with importers at the physical markets where they trade. You are meeting them one on one.

The importers you are talking to at those markets have the same problem every single day: they pay their Chinese, Indian, and Turkish suppliers upfront in dollars, wait three to six weeks for goods to arrive, and survive on thin margins the whole time. A bank will not lend them working capital because they have no credit history and no collateral the bank recognizes. But you could.

The move

A stablecoin-denominated supply chain credit line for African importers. They draw USDC from Daya, pay their supplier immediately, repay when the goods arrive and sell. Daya earns the spread and builds the most defensible moat in African fintech: a lending relationship with the businesses that move the most volume.

How it works, step by step

  1. Importer places an order with a Chinese supplier. Needs $15,000 in USDC to pay the invoice.
  2. Daya extends a $15,000 USDC credit line based on that invoice, a shipping document, and the importer's transaction history on the platform.
  3. Daya sends the USDC to the supplier. Settlement takes minutes. No correspondent bank. No SWIFT fee.
  4. Goods ship. Three to six weeks later they arrive at the Lagos port or the Kariakoo market.
  5. Importer sells the goods, converts the Naira proceeds back through Daya's rails, repays the credit. Daya takes a 2 to 3 percent spread.

The credit risk is real but it is also containable. You already know these importers. You see their transaction volume. You know their suppliers. The missing piece is the capital to lend and a few partners to make the pipeline airtight.

The logic of doing this now

Every African fintech is chasing payments. Very few are chasing credit because it is harder and scarier. But credit is where the margins live and it is where the defensibility lives. A payments relationship is transactional. A credit relationship is structural. The importer who owes you money also gives you every new transaction. They do not churn. They bring their friends.

You are already doing the hard part: showing up at the markets, knowing the importers by name, moving their money. The credit piece is a product decision and a capital decision. The product is clear. The capital comes from the partners above.

A $2.4M raise at pre-seed means you have runway to build the right thing, not the fast thing. Supply chain credit is the right thing.

I have been watching what Daya is doing and the thing that stands out is the operational grind. You are at the markets. You know the importers. That is rarer than the tech. The tech almost anyone can build. The trust at Alaba and Kariakoo takes years.
